Room Customiser

Designing a scalable room configuration system

Overview

Summary

Problem

Guests could only discover room preferences at the final booking step, and partners had no way to process requests at scale, limiting the feature's reach and impact.

My role

I delivered a rapid first iteration, and after positive A/B results, developed a longer-term solution letting guests customise their room, explore available options, and review their choices.

Impact

The result: A foundational step for our team and beyond.

Context

What are guest preferences?

Preferences are non-guaranteed customisations guests can request for their stay. Some apply at the room level — such as bed type or floor — while others apply at the property level, like check-in time or dietary needs. While not contractually guaranteed, they're a key driver of guest satisfaction.

Background

What do I meant by preferences?

Preferences are non-guaranteed customisations guests can request for their stay. Some apply at the room level — such as bed type or floor — while others apply at the property level, like check-in time or dietary needs.

While not contractually guaranteed, they're a key driver of guest satisfaction.

Research

What we knew going in.

Before we started designing the room customization system, we needed to have a clear understanding of what was working and what wasn't. For that, we conducted a comprehensive audit of existing features and user feedback.

What was working

Guests

Guests felt more in control by setting expectations upfront, boosting trust and satisfaction.

Partners

Partners saw a +1.3 uplift in review scores, fewer cancellations, and better pre-check-in preparedness.

Booking.com

More preference options drove higher engagement and incremental revenue.

What wasn't working

Guests hit a dead end

Preferences were only discoverable at the final booking step, via a free-text field — especially frustrating on mobile.

No scale for partners

Partners had to handle each guest request manually, with no way to process common ones at scale.

A broader opportunity

Other teams also wanted earlier access to preferences in the guest journey, signalling wider demand.

GOAL

My goal as a designer

  • Improved discoverability and reduced friction for guests
  • Minimised manual effort for partners
  • Transformed free-text requests into structured, actionable data
  • Enabled automation and cross-team integrations
  • Scaled across markets, property types, and future expansions

GOAL

The plan

First part of the plan was to bring the room related preferences to the context of room selection. That way, we would increase user awareness of the feature.

Partners were also complaining that unstructured requests too much time to reply, and ended up causing cancellations. So we wanted to bring structure to both sides. That's why I planned a way to let users select their preferences from a structured list, as it was already done in Desktop.

The original input text would still remain in it's plan in case users would like to add anything specific, but the idea was to increase the awareness of the features and promote available preferences for users while reducing partner workload.

Finally, there was the need to design a solution that could serve users with multiple preferences, not justone.

A family with 3 kids, for example, may choose the following setp: - 1 room with a double bed (bed type) and a cot (extra cot request) - 1 room with 2 single beds (bed type)

The plan was then presented to my team for feedback and approval. There were no major pushback except from the fact that the partner side on how to reply tO structured request was going to be handled by another team as it was out ouf our scope

Baseline

1. Bring customizations to Room Selection

When designing for A/B testing, clarity is essential: What's the smallest change that validates my hypothesis? Here, the minimal step wasn't letting users customize preferences directly—it was simply showing them that bed type options existed. This let us measure the impact of awareness alone, without introducing unnecessary complexity.

Design wise, the challenge was to present multiple bed options. While the most common case was for users to decide between a double bed and two single beds, we also needed to support cases like: 1 double bed + 1 single bed vs 3 single beds. In was in that cases that presenting the whole copy in a single line became problematic.

That's why, for the first design, I proposed to have every single bed option displayed in its own line, to improve readability.

Challenge

The challenge: more options, less noise

A single preference validated the model, but the real goal was to surface a broader range without overwhelming guests or cluttering the room selection experience. More options meant more complexity. The question became: how do we scale the preference surface while keeping the interaction feel lightweight?

Insight

A new screen is needed

After testing multiple approaches within the room card, it became clear that a dedicated screen was the right path: one that could scale the preference experience without compromising the room selection flow.

Guest Research · 2023

What guests actually want
when booking a room.

Across 18 guest interviews and session recordings, four themes shaped every design decision.

1 Choice Overload

Guests abandoned the booking flow when presented with more than 3 customization options at once.

"I just want a nice room — why does it feel like a spreadsheet?"

2 Mobile-First Decisions

Over 70% of room selections happened on mobile, yet the customization UI was designed for desktop.

"The buttons are tiny. I just pick whatever and move on."

3 Visuals Over Labels

Guests trusted room photos far more than text descriptions when choosing between options.

"Show me what it looks like — I don't read the descriptions."

4 Late-Stage Dropout

Most drop-offs happened right before confirmation — guests lost track of what they'd selected.

"I got confused about what I'd actually chosen."
